Alumni Profiles

Hear from Liverpool Hope University psychology graduates about the careers they have built, the routes they took, and their advice for current students.

Mental Health Practitioner / PWP
Alice Keenan
Degree: BA(Hons) Psychology and Music Graduated: 2022 Current role: Mental Health Practitioner/PWP, Alder Hey Children's Hospital, Eating Disorder Team

I completed a BA(Hons) in Psychology and Music and graduated in 2022. After graduation I completed a MSc in Applied Clinical Psychology whilst working as a mental health support worker. I then completed a Post Graduate Certificate as a part of my psychological wellbeing practitioner training. I worked as a qualified PWP in NHS Talking Therapies for a year after finishing training, before accepting a job as a mental health practitioner/PWP at Alder Hey Children's Hospital in the eating disorder team.


I would advise undergraduates to really look into Psychology as a career and make sure that they are taking the right path. A psychologist is more than a therapist, and there is a lot of managerial work and audit involved which can take away from clinical time with patients. However, if this is right for you then go for it and try to get experience aligned with the doctoral programme you wish to pursue whether that be clinical, forensic, educational, occupational, counselling etc.

Advice to current students

Remember that there are more careers in the psychological professions than just the doctorate and there are loads of options out there. I think it is helpful to know about the range of options out there, and also the strengths and limitations of each choice as it can be a big decision!

Data Consultant
Caroline Knowles
Degree: BSc Psychology (MSc Research Methods in Psychology) Graduated: 2024 Current role: Data Consultant

I graduated in July 2024. Since graduation I have completed a Master's in Research Methods in Psychology where I was a Student Representative and have just finished a year as a Data Consultant. My degree has taught me the importance of good data, understanding and communicating research results to a range of audiences while also building my soft skills of teamwork, patience and time management (arguably more important!). The most important transferable skill I built during university which I find is most valuable to employers is communication, this is the basis of everything in the workplace. Next, is confidence. Moving away from home at 18 into a scene where everything is self-motivated, having the confidence to get out of your comfort zone builds up valuable skills that employers love. In my field employers look for Presentation skills, public speaking, data management skills, computer skills. While the Tech industry is dominated by developers and solution architects, people with a psychology background really stand out in bringing the people focus. Psychology offers such a broad range of skills that can feel so difficult to box up into a job.

Advice to current students

I think it is really important to focus on a few things that you enjoy and explore them, taking time to understand what you want to get out of your career.

Researcher / Academic
Eleanor Rose
Degree: BSc Psychology Graduated: 2017 Current role: Academia

I graduated from my Psychology degree in 2017. Since graduating I have done everything I wanted to! I tried many career paths from Samaritans, mental health and youth advocacy, NHS jobs, third sector roles, private sector and landed happily back in academia where I "Hope" to stay.


I would not call my degree a stepping stone because that is not accurate. It is the theoretical basis for all the skills, knowledge and applied learning you will need as a Hope Graduate for the rest of your working life. Your degree can become whatever you want it to be; Everywhere needs a human touch so Psychology will always be relevant.


Employers value knowledge about stats… (Yeah, we all hate it at first!)

Advice to current students

As soon as you learn how to think like a statistician, especially first thing on a Friday morning, you can do anything you want to do. My advice is to take the fixed term contracts, travel and say yes to every exciting opportunity!

Clinical Psychologist
Anonymous
Degree: Conversion MSc in Psychology Graduated: 2013 Current role: Assistant Clinical Lead

I completed a conversion MSc in Psychology and graduated in 2013. Shortly after graduating I got my first job as an Assistant Psychologist. I then had two further Assistant Psychologist roles. I was accepted onto the Doctorate of Clinical Psychology at the University of Liverpool and graduated in February 2020. Since then, I have worked as a Clinical Psychologist. I completed further training in Cognitive Analytic Therapy (CAT) qualifying as a CAT practitioner in June 2025. Recently, I have moved into an Assistant Clinical Lead position at the service I work at.

Advice to current students

My advice for anybody looking to go into the field of Clinical Psychology is to try to gain some experience working with people who have struggles with mental health — this could be volunteering. Whilst doing so, think about the application of the psychological theory you are learning to this context. Also find out as much about the job as possible, including the strengths and challenges of the role, to see if it truly is for you.
