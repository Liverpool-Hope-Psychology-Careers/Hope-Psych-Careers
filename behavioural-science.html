Behavioural Science and Behaviour Change

Applying psychological and behavioural economics principles to help organisations and governments understand and influence decision-making.

One of the most psychology-specific non-clinical career areas, behavioural science applies insights from psychology and behavioural economics to understand and influence human behaviour. From public health campaigns to sustainability initiatives and government policy, this field is growing rapidly — and many students are unaware it exists as a career pathway.

What you could do now
  • Read the Behavioural Insights Team's EAST framework — a practical introduction to applied behavioural science
  • Explore modules on social influence, decision-making, and health psychology in your degree
  • Look into internship opportunities at the Behavioural Insights Team and similar organisations
  • Consider volunteering with public health or sustainability campaigns to build applied experience
  • Research postgraduate programmes in behavioural science or health psychology if you want to specialise

Public Behaviour Change

Public behaviour change roles design and deliver interventions to encourage healthier, safer, or more sustainable behaviour across communities and populations. These roles draw directly on psychological theory and evidence, making them an excellent fit for psychology graduates.

Why Psychology?
  • Deep knowledge of behaviour change theory — COM-B, the Transtheoretical Model, nudge theory, and others — directly underpins this work
  • Understanding of motivation, habit formation, and barriers to behaviour change
  • Research and evaluation skills for designing and testing interventions
  • Communication skills for engaging diverse communities and stakeholders
  • Awareness of health inequalities and the social determinants of behaviour
Possible roles in this area
Behaviour Change Officer
Designs and delivers interventions to change public behaviour around health, safety, sustainability, or other priorities, drawing on psychological theory.
Graduate entry
Public Health Behaviour Change Specialist
Develops and delivers behaviour change programmes in public health contexts, such as smoking cessation, physical activity promotion, or healthy eating.
Graduate entry
Sustainability Behaviour Change Officer
Applies psychological principles to encourage pro-environmental behaviour in organisations, communities, or the general public.
Graduate entry

Behavioural Science

Behavioural science careers involve researching and applying insights about human behaviour, often working at the intersection of psychology, economics, and policy. This field emerged largely from academic psychology and remains one of the most directly psychology-relevant career areas available to graduates.

Why Psychology?
  • Behavioural science is grounded in psychological research — cognitive biases, social norms, heuristics, and decision-making theory are all central
  • Research design and statistical skills are essential for running experiments and evaluating interventions
  • Critical thinking skills help behavioural scientists design rigorous studies and avoid common analytical errors
  • Understanding of motivation and behaviour change theory informs practical intervention design
  • Ethical awareness around behavioural influence is increasingly important in the field
Possible roles in this area
Behavioural Scientist
Uses insights from psychology and behavioural economics to understand and influence human behaviour in commercial, public sector, or policy settings.
Graduate entry
Behavioural Insights Practitioner
Works within government, NHS, or private sector teams to apply behavioural science to policy and practice — designing trials, analysing results, and translating findings into recommendations.
Graduate entry
Behavioural Research Associate
Conducts research to understand behaviour and test the effectiveness of interventions, often in a consultancy or think tank setting.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Behavioural science knowledge Research design and statistics Data analysis Communication of evidence Critical thinking Ethical reasoning
Example employers
  • Behavioural Insights Team and similar applied behavioural science consultancies
  • Government departments including HMRC, DWP, and Cabinet Office
  • Financial Conduct Authority and other regulators using behavioural approaches
  • Technology companies and financial services organisations with behavioural science teams
What you could do now
  • Read Thinking, Fast and Slow by Daniel Kahneman — a foundational text for understanding behavioural science
  • Explore the Behavioural Insights Team website and their published trials and reports
  • Look for internship opportunities at the BIT and similar organisations
  • Build strong research and statistical skills through your degree — these are essential for practitioner roles
