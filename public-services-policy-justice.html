Public Services, Policy and Justice

Civil service, policy, social work and justice — careers where psychological knowledge informs public services and improves outcomes for people.

Psychology graduates contribute to a wide range of public sector careers, from government policy and intelligence to public health and criminal justice. Analytical skills, understanding of human behaviour, and research training are all highly valued in this sector — and psychology graduates are often stronger candidates than they realise.

What you could do now
  • Research the Civil Service Fast Stream — applications open annually and psychology graduates are competitive candidates
  • Look into the NHS Graduate Management Training Scheme
  • Volunteer with community support organisations to build relevant experience
  • Explore the Government Social Research profession for research-focused roles
  • Attend public sector careers events and employer talks

Government and Policy

Government and policy careers draw on analytical thinking, research skills, and understanding of human behaviour to develop and evaluate public policy. Psychology graduates are often strong candidates for government roles — particularly those requiring evidence synthesis, communication, and behavioural insight.

Why Psychology?
  • Research and analytical skills are central to policy development and evaluation — exactly what psychology training provides
  • Understanding of human behaviour, decision-making, and social influence informs how policy is designed and communicated
  • Behavioural insights are now embedded across many government departments, making psychology graduates particularly relevant
  • Strong written communication skills for producing briefings, reports, and policy papers
  • Critical thinking and evidence evaluation skills essential for a career in government
Possible roles in this area
Civil Service Fast Stream
A highly competitive accelerated leadership programme across government departments. Open to all graduates and particularly suited to psychology students with strong analytical and communication skills.
Graduate entry
Policy Adviser
Uses research and analytical skills to develop and evaluate government policy across areas including health, education, justice, and the economy.
Graduate entry
Government Social Researcher
Conducts research for government departments to inform policy, service design, and public communications, using a range of qualitative and quantitative methods.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Analytical thinking Research methods Written communication Evidence synthesis Understanding of behaviour Attention to detail
Example employers
  • Civil Service departments including Home Office, DWP, DHSC, and Cabinet Office
  • Government Social Research profession across all departments
  • NHS England and NHS Improvement
  • Local authorities and Combined Authorities with policy functions
What you could do now
  • Research the Civil Service Fast Stream and apply — applications typically open in September
  • Look into the Government Social Research profession and its entry requirements
  • Practise the analytical and written exercises used in Civil Service recruitment
  • Consider student placements in local government or parliamentary settings

Intelligence and Security

Intelligence and security roles draw on psychological understanding of behaviour, motivation, and decision-making to support national security and fraud prevention. These are less obvious pathways for psychology graduates but ones where the degree is directly and distinctively relevant.

Why Psychology?
  • Understanding of human behaviour, motivation, and decision-making is central to intelligence analysis
  • Knowledge of cognitive biases and how they affect judgement is directly applicable to intelligence work
  • Analytical and research skills for synthesising complex information from multiple sources
  • Understanding of social influence, group dynamics, and radicalisation processes
  • Ethical awareness and professional discretion developed throughout the degree
Possible roles in this area
Intelligence Analyst
Analyses information to identify threats and support national security operations, drawing on psychological understanding of behaviour and decision-making.
Graduate entry
Counter-Fraud Analyst
Uses behavioural and analytical skills to detect patterns of fraudulent activity and support prevention work across public and private sector organisations.
Graduate entry
Security Research Officer
Conducts research to support intelligence and security operations, drawing on social science methods and psychological theory.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Analytical thinking Understanding of behaviour and motivation Research and evidence synthesis Written communication Attention to detail Discretion and ethical awareness
Example employers
  • GCHQ, MI5, and MI6 — all recruit graduates including from psychology
  • National Crime Agency
  • Police forces and specialist units
  • Department for Work and Pensions and HMRC counter-fraud teams
What you could do now
  • Research graduate roles at GCHQ, MI5, and the National Crime Agency — all have public graduate recruitment
  • Look into the Civil Service Fast Stream analytical and intelligence streams
  • Build strong analytical and research skills through your degree and dissertation
  • Be aware that many roles require security clearance — honesty and integrity in your application is essential

Public Health

Public health careers focus on improving population health through evidence-based programmes, policy, and behaviour change. Psychology graduates are well equipped for this field, bringing research skills, behavioural expertise, and understanding of health inequalities.

Why Psychology?
  • Behaviour change theory — central to public health — is taught throughout psychology degrees
  • Research and evaluation skills for designing and assessing health interventions
  • Understanding of health inequalities and the psychological and social factors that influence health
  • Communication skills for engaging diverse communities in health programmes
  • Familiarity with mixed methods research — both qualitative and quantitative approaches are used in public health
Possible roles in this area
Public Health Practitioner
Works to improve population health through evidence-based programmes, campaigns, and policy recommendations at local or national level.
Graduate entry
Health Improvement Officer
Designs and delivers programmes to promote healthier lifestyles and reduce health inequalities, drawing on behaviour change theory and community engagement.
Graduate entry
Public Health Intelligence Analyst
Analyses population health data to identify trends, inform strategy, and support public health decision-making.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Behaviour change knowledge Research and evaluation Data analysis Community engagement Written communication Partnership working
Example employers
  • NHS Trusts and Integrated Care Boards
  • Local authority public health teams
  • UK Health Security Agency
  • Office for Health Improvements and Disparities (OHID)
What you could do now
  • Research the NHS Graduate Management Training Scheme — public health is one of its specialisms
  • Volunteer with health charities or community health projects
  • Explore health psychology and behaviour change modules in your degree
  • Look into the Faculty of Public Health and routes into specialist public health training

Social Services and Criminal Justice

Social services and criminal justice roles involve supporting vulnerable individuals and contributing to rehabilitation, safeguarding, and community safety. Psychology graduates are well suited to this sector, though some roles require further professional training.

Why Psychology?
  • Understanding of behaviour, risk, and the factors that contribute to offending and vulnerability
  • Knowledge of attachment theory, developmental psychology, and family dynamics relevant to social work
  • Communication and empathy skills for working with individuals in difficult circumstances
  • Understanding of rehabilitation psychology and evidence-based approaches to reducing reoffending
  • Ethical awareness and professional boundaries essential in frontline roles
Possible roles in this area
Community Support Worker
Supports individuals in the community with daily living, often working with people with mental health difficulties, learning disabilities, or other vulnerabilities.
Graduate entry
Prison Officer
Works within custodial settings, drawing on understanding of behaviour, risk, and rehabilitation to support safe and effective custody.
Graduate entry
Probation Officer (with training)
Works with offenders to support rehabilitation and manage risk in the community. Requires completion of the Probation Qualification Framework.
Professional training required
Social Worker (with further training)
Works with vulnerable individuals and families to safeguard and support wellbeing. Requires a Social Work degree or postgraduate qualification.
Postgrad training required
Skills that may be useful
Empathy and communication Understanding of risk and behaviour Professional boundaries Resilience Report writing Safeguarding awareness
Example employers
  • His Majesty's Prison and Probation Service
  • Local authority children's and adults' social care teams
  • Charities working in criminal justice and rehabilitation
  • Community interest companies and voluntary sector organisations
What you could do now
  • Volunteer with a charity working in criminal justice or social care to build experience
  • Research the Probation Qualification Framework and how to enter the probation service
  • Look into care and support work as part-time employment — relevant experience and paid
  • Research postgraduate Social Work routes if that is your longer-term aim
