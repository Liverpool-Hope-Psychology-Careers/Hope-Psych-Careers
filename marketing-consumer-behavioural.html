Marketing, Consumer Insight and Behavioural Insight

Marketing, consumer research and applied behavioural science — careers where understanding how people think and make decisions is commercially valued.

An understanding of how people think, make decisions, and respond to persuasion makes psychology graduates particularly well suited to careers in marketing, consumer research, and behavioural insight. These are some of the most commercially valued areas for psychology graduates and are often overlooked as a career pathway.

What you could do now
  • Build a LinkedIn profile and follow marketing and consumer insight employers
  • Look into graduate schemes at agencies such as Ipsos, Kantar, and YouGov
  • Research the Market Research Society and its student resources
  • Consider modules covering social influence, persuasion, and consumer behaviour in your degree
  • Look for marketing assistant roles as part-time work to build commercial awareness

Marketing and Advertising

Marketing and advertising draw heavily on psychological principles of attention, persuasion, memory, and consumer behaviour. Psychology graduates bring a distinctive analytical perspective that many employers value alongside creative thinking.

Why Psychology?
  • Deep understanding of persuasion, influence, and how people respond to messages — central to effective marketing
  • Knowledge of attention, memory, and perception informs how advertising is designed and placed
  • Research and analytical skills for measuring campaign effectiveness and audience insight
  • Understanding of identity, social influence, and group behaviour relevant to brand strategy
  • Ethical awareness around nudging, persuasion, and consumer autonomy — increasingly valued in the sector

Uses psychological insight into perception, identity, and persuasion to develop brand strategy and communications.
Graduate entry
Media Planner
Understands audience psychology to inform how and where advertising is placed for maximum impact.
Graduate entry
Content Strategist
Uses knowledge of attention, memory, and persuasion to develop content that engages audiences and drives behaviour.

Consumer Research

Consumer research roles involve investigating how and why people make purchasing decisions, applying psychological research methods in commercial settings. This is one of the most natural fits for psychology graduates entering the commercial world.

Why Psychology?
  • Training in both quantitative and qualitative research methods — exactly what consumer research employers need
  • Understanding of attitude formation, decision-making, and behaviour change directly informs consumer insight work
  • Experience with surveys, interviews, and data analysis from degree assessments and dissertation
  • Ability to synthesise complex data and communicate findings clearly to non-specialist audiences
  • Ethical awareness around research with human participants
Possible roles in this area
Market Research Executive
Designs and conducts qualitative and quantitative research to understand consumer attitudes, behaviours, and preferences for commercial clients.
Graduate entry
Consumer Insights Analyst
Analyses data from surveys, focus groups, and sales figures to generate insights about consumer behaviour and inform business strategy.
Graduate entry
Qualitative Research Executive
Plans and moderates focus groups and depth interviews to explore consumer attitudes and motivations in depth.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Quantitative and qualitative methods Survey design Data analysis Report writing Interviewing and facilitation Commercial awareness
Example employers
  • Market research agencies including Ipsos, Kantar, YouGov, and Savanta
  • In-house insight teams at large retailers, FMCG brands, and financial services
  • Advertising and brand consultancies with research functions
  • Public sector and charity organisations conducting audience research
What you could do now
  • Research graduate schemes at major market research agencies — many recruit directly from psychology
  • Explore Market Research Society student membership and resources
  • Practise running focus groups and interviews through degree projects
  • Build familiarity with survey platforms like Qualtrics through your degree work

Behavioural Insight

Behavioural insight roles apply psychological and behavioural economics principles to help organisations understand and influence decision-making — in commercial, public sector, and policy contexts. This is one of the most psychology-specific career areas and is growing rapidly.

Why Psychology?
  • Behavioural insight is built directly on psychology — drawing on cognitive biases, heuristics, social norms, and decision-making theory
  • Research methods training is essential for designing and evaluating behavioural interventions
  • Understanding of motivation, habit, and behaviour change informs nudge-based approaches
  • Analytical skills for interpreting data and communicating evidence to clients and stakeholders
  • Critical thinking around the ethics of nudging and behavioural influence
Possible roles in this area
Behavioural Insights Officer
Applies behavioural science to design interventions that influence decision-making in public or private sector contexts, often running trials to test effectiveness.
Graduate entry
Nudge Specialist
Uses knowledge of behavioural economics and psychology to design choice environments that make desired behaviours easier and more likely.
Graduate entry
Behavioural Science Consultant
Advises organisations on how to apply behavioural insights to improve outcomes — from employee engagement to customer behaviour and public policy.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Behavioural science knowledge Research and evaluation Analytical thinking Communication of evidence Ethical reasoning Project management
Example employers
  • Behavioural Insights Team and similar applied behavioural science organisations
  • Government departments and regulators using behavioural approaches
  • Management consultancies with behavioural science practices
  • Financial services, utilities, and tech companies applying behavioural insight to products
What you could do now
  • Read the Behavioural Insights Team's EAST framework — a practical introduction to applied behavioural science
  • Look into internship opportunities at the Behavioural Insights Team and similar organisations
  • Explore modules on social influence, decision-making, and behaviour change in your degree
  • Research postgraduate programmes in behavioural science if you want to specialise further
