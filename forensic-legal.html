Forensic and Legal Psychology

Criminal justice, offender management, risk assessment and legal processes — careers applying psychological knowledge to public protection and rehabilitation.

Forensic and legal psychology applies psychological knowledge to criminal justice, offender rehabilitation, risk assessment, legal processes and public protection. There are graduate-entry routes into this field, particularly in offender management and criminal justice support roles, alongside specialist practitioner routes that require further postgraduate training.

Note on scope: This page covers criminal justice and policy-related careers. Clinical forensic psychology — including qualified forensic psychologist roles — involves further training and falls under the further training routes covered elsewhere on this site.

Criminal Justice and Offender Management

Roles in criminal justice and offender management involve working with people who have offended or are at risk of offending, supporting rehabilitation, managing risk, and contributing to public protection. Psychology graduates can enter this field directly at graduate level through HMPPS and probation graduate schemes.

Why psychology?
  • Understanding of risk factors, mental health and behaviour change underpins effective offender supervision
  • Psychological theories of motivation and desistance inform rehabilitation programmes
  • Strong communication and professional boundary skills are essential to working in high-stakes settings
Roles in Criminal Justice and Offender Management
Prison Officer / Offender Supervisor
Works with people in custody to support sentence management, behaviour change and preparation for release. Psychology graduates are valued for their understanding of behaviour and communication skills.
Graduate entry
Probation Officer (Trainee)
Supervises offenders in the community, produces pre-sentence reports and assesses risk. The Professional Qualification in Probation (PQiP) is completed on the job.
Training on the job
Assistant Psychologist (HMPPS)
Supports qualified forensic psychologists in delivering and evaluating offending behaviour programmes within the prison service.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Risk assessment Professional boundaries Report writing Resilience Empathy Ethical practice
Example employers
  • His Majesty's Prison and Probation Service (HMPPS)
  • National Probation Service
  • Youth Offending Teams (local authority)
  • Charities supporting people in the justice system (Nacro, St Giles Trust, St Mungo's)

Forensic Assessment and Expert Work

Forensic assessment involves psychological evaluation of individuals involved in legal proceedings — including risk assessment, cognitive assessment and expert witness work. Many of the senior roles in this area require postgraduate training and registration as a forensic psychologist, but psychology graduates can enter supporting roles at assistant level.

Further training note: Becoming a Chartered Forensic Psychologist requires Stage 2 training following a BPS-accredited postgraduate qualification. The roles below represent the graduate-entry pathway into this field.
Why psychology?
  • Training in psychological assessment tools and research methods directly transfers to forensic evaluation work
  • Understanding of psychopathology, risk factors and cognitive processes underpins forensic report writing
  • Ethical training equips psychology graduates to navigate the complex duty-of-care issues in legal settings
Roles in Forensic Assessment and Expert Work
Assistant Psychologist (Forensic)
Supports qualified forensic psychologists in assessment, report writing and intervention delivery in secure settings or the community.
Graduate entry
Psychological Wellbeing Practitioner (Forensic)
Delivers low-intensity CBT-based interventions to people in the criminal justice system with mental health needs — typically via an IAPT-style training pathway.
Additional training needed
Chartered Forensic Psychologist
Conducts risk assessments, provides expert opinion to courts and tribunals, and develops treatment programmes for offenders. Requires Stage 2 qualification and HCPC registration.
Postgrad required
Skills that may be useful
Psychological assessment Report writing Analytical thinking Ethical judgement Attention to detail Professional communication
Example employers
  • HMPPS and secure psychiatric hospitals
  • NHS forensic mental health services
  • Independent forensic psychology practices
  • Courts, tribunals and legal teams

Policy, Research and Crime Analysis

Psychology graduates can apply their research and analytical skills to criminal justice policy, crime prevention and public safety. These roles sit within government departments, police forces, research institutes and think tanks, and are open to graduates with strong quantitative or qualitative research skills.

Why psychology?
  • Research methods training equips psychology graduates to design and analyse criminal justice evaluations
  • Understanding of behaviour, risk and cognition informs evidence-based crime prevention and policy work
  • Strong critical thinking and written communication skills are highly valued in policy and research settings
Roles in Policy, Research and Crime Analysis
Crime Analyst
Analyses crime data and patterns to inform policing priorities, resource allocation and crime prevention strategies within police forces or local authorities.
Graduate entry
Criminal Justice Policy Researcher
Conducts research and produces evidence reviews to inform legislation, policy development and programme evaluation within government or research bodies.
Graduate entry
Research Officer (Criminology / Justice)
Designs and delivers research projects examining offending, victimisation, rehabilitation or policing effectiveness for academic or policy organisations.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Research methods Data analysis Written communication Critical thinking Policy awareness Attention to detail
Example employers
  • Home Office and Ministry of Justice research teams
  • Police forces and the College of Policing
  • Think tanks and research institutes (e.g. Policy Exchange, Crest Advisory)
  • Universities with criminology and forensic psychology departments
