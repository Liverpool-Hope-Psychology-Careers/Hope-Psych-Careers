Charity and Third Sector

The voluntary and community sector — careers in charities, social enterprises and community organisations where psychological insight supports people in need.

The voluntary sector offers a wide range of roles where psychological knowledge of human behaviour, motivation, and wellbeing is directly applicable. From project coordination and fundraising to research, evaluation, and advocacy, the third sector is a significant graduate employer — and one that values the skills psychology graduates bring.

What you could do now
  • Volunteer with a local charity — many small organisations need support with coordination, research, and communications
  • Look into CharityJob.co.uk for graduate roles in the voluntary sector
  • Research the NCVO and its resources on careers in the voluntary sector
  • Consider a student ambassador or peer mentor role to build relevant skills
  • Look for charity internship programmes run by organisations such as the Social Mobility Foundation

Charity Work

Charities offer a wide range of operational roles for psychology graduates — from coordinating projects and managing volunteers to fundraising and communications. These roles draw on interpersonal skills, understanding of motivation, and the ability to work across diverse communities.

Why Psychology?
  • Understanding of human motivation and behaviour informs effective volunteer management and donor engagement
  • Communication and interpersonal skills for working with beneficiaries, staff, and funders
  • Knowledge of group dynamics and team behaviour relevant to coordinating volunteers and project teams
  • Analytical skills for reporting on outputs and outcomes to funders
  • Empathy and ethical awareness essential for working with vulnerable people and communities
Possible roles in this area
Charity Project Coordinator
Manages projects and programmes in the voluntary sector, often focused on social issues such as health, poverty, education, or community development.
Graduate entry
Fundraising and Development Officer
Uses communication and persuasion skills to secure funding and support for charitable causes, through trusts, individual giving, and events.
Graduate entry
Volunteer Coordinator
Recruits, trains, and supports volunteers, drawing on understanding of motivation and group dynamics to build effective volunteer communities.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Communication Organisation and project management Empathy Understanding of motivation Fundraising and bid writing Stakeholder management
Example employers
  • Large national charities including Mind, Age UK, Shelter, and the British Red Cross
  • Local and regional charities across all cause areas
  • Community interest companies and social enterprises
  • Housing associations and community organisations

Advocacy and Campaigning

Advocacy and campaigning roles involve developing and delivering campaigns to influence public opinion and policy on social issues. Psychology graduates bring a distinctive understanding of persuasion, attitude change, and behaviour that is directly relevant to this work.

Why Psychology?
  • Deep understanding of persuasion, attitude formation, and behaviour change — central to effective campaigning
  • Knowledge of social norms and group influence informs how campaigns are framed and targeted
  • Research skills for building an evidence base for advocacy positions
  • Written and verbal communication skills for creating compelling campaign materials
  • Understanding of media, public opinion, and the psychology of political engagement
Possible roles in this area
Policy and Campaigns Officer
Develops and delivers campaigns on social issues, engaging with politicians, media, and the public to drive policy change on behalf of a charity or campaign organisation.
Graduate entry
Communications Officer
Manages an organisation's communications and media presence, using understanding of audience psychology to craft effective messages across different channels.
Graduate entry
Public Affairs Officer
Engages with government, parliament, and stakeholders on behalf of an organisation to influence policy and legislation, drawing on research and communication skills.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Persuasion and influence Written communication Research and evidence use Political awareness Media and digital skills Stakeholder engagement
Example employers
  • Health and social care charities including Cancer Research UK, Mind, and Scope
  • Environmental organisations including Friends of the Earth and WWF
  • Human rights and equality organisations
  • Trade associations and professional bodies with public affairs functions

Programme Evaluation

Evaluation roles in the charity sector assess the impact of programmes and interventions, using research skills to demonstrate value to funders and inform future development. Psychology graduates are well placed for these roles given their training in research design and data analysis.

Why Psychology?
  • Research methods training provides the foundation for designing and conducting programme evaluations
  • Knowledge of psychological theory helps evaluators understand why interventions do or do not work
  • Both qualitative and quantitative data collection and analysis skills are directly applicable
  • Understanding of behaviour change theory is particularly relevant in health and social care evaluation
  • Ability to communicate complex findings clearly to commissioners, funders, and the public
Possible roles in this area
Impact Officer
Measures and reports on the outcomes and social impact of a charity's programmes, often producing annual impact reports and case studies for funders.
Graduate entry
Monitoring and Evaluation Officer
Tracks programme delivery and outcomes, collecting and analysing data to support learning and accountability to funders and beneficiaries.
Graduate entry
Research Officer
Conducts research to inform strategy, advocacy, and programme development within a charity, drawing on both desk research and primary data collection.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Research methods Data collection and analysis Report writing Communication of findings Understanding of behaviour change Attention to detail
Example employers
  • Large charities with dedicated impact and evaluation teams
  • Social research consultancies working with the voluntary sector
  • Foundations and grant-making bodies requiring evidence of impact
  • NHS and public health bodies evaluating community health programmes
