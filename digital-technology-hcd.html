Digital, Technology and Human-Centred Design

UX research, human factors and digital product design — careers where understanding human behaviour shapes the technology people use every day.

Psychology graduates bring a deep understanding of how people think, behave, and interact with technology — making them well suited to careers in UX research, human factors, and digital health. These roles sit at the intersection of psychology and technology, and are increasingly in demand as digital products and services become central to everyday life.

Sub-themes
What you could do right now
  • Complete a free UX or human-computer interaction course (Google UX Design on Coursera, or Nielsen Norman Group resources)
  • Build a basic portfolio — document a small usability test or redesign of a student app
  • Volunteer for psychology research studies to understand participant experience from the inside
  • Follow the British Computer Society and CIEHF for student membership and events
  • Explore graduate programmes at NHS Digital, GCHQ, and major tech firms targeting psychology graduates

UX and User Research

UX (user experience) researchers study how people interact with products, services and systems to help designers make better decisions. Psychology graduates are well suited to this field because their training in research methods, cognitive processes, and human behaviour directly applies to understanding user needs.

Why psychology?
  • Research methods training — surveys, interviews, observations — maps directly to UX research techniques
  • Understanding of cognitive load, attention, memory and decision-making informs usability evaluation
  • Psychological theories of motivation and behaviour inform design for engagement and accessibility
Roles in UX and User Research
UX Researcher
Plans and conducts user interviews, usability tests and surveys to understand how people experience products and identify design improvements.
Graduate entry
Junior UX Designer
Uses research insights to prototype and iterate on product designs, working closely with developers and product managers.
Graduate entry
Insight Analyst
Analyses user data, feedback and behavioural metrics to surface patterns and recommendations for product teams.
Graduate entry

Human Factors and Ergonomics

Human factors specialists design systems, environments and equipment to fit human capability, reducing error and improving performance and safety. This field draws heavily on cognitive and occupational psychology and operates across aviation, healthcare, defence, transport and consumer technology.

Why psychology?
  • Cognitive psychology — how people process information, make decisions, and make errors — is central to human factors work
  • Understanding workload, stress, and fatigue informs the design of safer working environments
  • Research and evaluation skills support evidence-based system design and incident analysis
Roles in Human Factors and Ergonomics
Human Factors Specialist
Evaluates how people interact with equipment, interfaces and procedures, and recommends design changes to reduce risk and improve performance.
Graduate entry
Ergonomics Consultant
Advises organisations on workspace design, equipment layout and working practices to minimise physical and cognitive strain.
Graduate entry
Safety and Human Performance Analyst
Investigates incidents and near-misses in safety-critical industries, identifying human error contributions and systemic improvements.
Graduate entry

Digital Health and Assistive Technology

Digital health roles involve developing, evaluating and implementing technology that supports people's health, wellbeing and independence. This includes mental health apps, assistive devices, telehealth platforms, and digital therapeutics. Psychology graduates bring behaviour change expertise and a person-centred perspective essential to this sector.

Why psychology?
  • Behaviour change models and psychological theory underpin effective health technology design
  • Understanding user needs, barriers and motivations is essential to digital health product development
  • Evaluation and research skills are needed to assess whether digital tools actually improve outcomes
Roles in Digital Health and Assistive Technology
Digital Health Coordinator
Supports the implementation and evaluation of digital health programmes within NHS trusts or public health organisations.
Graduate entry
Assistive Technology Specialist
Assesses individuals' needs and recommends or trains them to use technology that supports their independence and daily functioning.
Graduate entry
Digital Therapeutics Research Associate
Supports clinical trials and evaluation studies testing whether digital health interventions produce genuine behavioural and health outcomes.
Graduate entry
