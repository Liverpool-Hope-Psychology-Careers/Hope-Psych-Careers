Graduate Outcomes, Employer Trends and Practical Support

What the evidence actually says about where psychology graduates go, what careers count as psychology-related, and what employers look for — alongside practical tools to help you prepare.

The most important thing to understand
Most psychology students think there is one path. The evidence shows there are thousands.

Research by the Nuffield Trust found that 91% of psychology undergraduates want a career in mental health at the outset of their degree — most specifically aiming for clinical psychology. By final year that figure falls to 79%, but still far exceeds the proportion who will reach registered clinical roles.

The gap is largely one of awareness. Of around 20,300 psychology graduates per year, roughly 12,000 go on to work in roles directly related to psychology — but only around 1,300 as registered psychologists. Around 10,700 are working in psychology-relevant careers outside the registered professions, in areas many students simply have not considered.

The career pathways on this site map to where those graduates actually go.

What the data shows at 15 months

Graduate Outcomes: The Reality

National data on where psychology graduates end up is typically measured 15 months after graduation. It is a useful snapshot but significantly underestimates long-term psychology career progression — the BPS notes it typically takes at least three years post-graduation to reach the intended career destination.

The 15-month snapshot understates progression. Around 29% of psychology graduates have a "not known or not applicable" employment status at 6 months after graduation — comparable to sociology, but far higher than medicine or nursing. Many are still finding their path, completing further study, or in roles that will lead somewhere more relevant. The BPS survey shows that over time graduates consistently move towards more psychology-relevant roles — being in a non-professional role early on is not an indicator of being off-track.

On the healthcare figure: Only 27% of psychology graduates are working in healthcare professions 15 months after graduation, compared to the 91% who wanted a mental health career at the start of their degree. But healthcare is one sector among many. Education, welfare, research, business, the civil service, charity, digital and HR all employ large numbers of psychology graduates in directly relevant roles not captured in that figure. The Nuffield Trust explicitly notes that the mismatch between aspiration and 15-month outcomes reflects limited awareness of options, not failure.
For those aiming for clinical psychology specifically

The Clinical Route: What Students Need to Know

Clinical psychology is the most popular aspiration among psychology undergraduates and one of the most competitive routes in any profession. Understanding this early allows you to plan properly rather than be caught off guard.

Only 1 in 6 applicants gets a place
Fewer than one in six applicants (15%) gained a place on the Clinical Psychology Doctorate in 2019. Many applicants apply multiple times over several years before being accepted or changing direction.
Nuffield Trust, 2021
4 in 5 doctoral candidates had 3 or more years of experience
Four in five people enrolling on the Clinical Psychology Doctorate in 2019 had at least three years of relevant work experience. Building that experience during and after your degree is not optional — it is the route.
Nuffield Trust, 2021
Only 4% of undergraduates do work experience during their degree
A survey of psychology students who aspired to mental health careers found that only 4% were actually undertaking work experience during their degree (National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2019, cited in Nuffield Trust). This figure relates specifically to that group, but the broader message is consistent with wider evidence — work experience during a psychology degree is significantly underutilised relative to how important it is for competitive career routes.
National Collaborating Centre for Mental Health, 2019, cited in Nuffield Trust
Around 6% of all graduates become registered psychologists
The Nuffield Trust estimates fewer than one in 15 psychology graduates become registered professionals. For the other 94%, a fulfilling psychology-relevant career still exists — just not in a registered clinical role.
Nuffield Trust, 2021

What this means for you: If clinical psychology is your goal, start building relevant experience now — not after graduation. If you are not yet sure, explore the full breadth of career options on this site before narrowing your focus. The skills your degree builds — research, analytical thinking, empathy, communication, ethical reasoning — are valued across all career pathways, not just the clinical route.

What employers are looking for in 2025

Skills Employers Value

Across sectors, employers consistently identify the same gaps in graduate applicants — and they are skills that psychology degrees build directly. The challenge is knowing how to demonstrate them with specific evidence.

Problem solving and analytical thinking
CIPD research identifies problem solving as one of the skills employers struggle most to find. Psychology graduates trained in evaluating evidence and reasoning through complexity are well placed — but need to articulate this with specific examples, not just claim it.
CIPD Employer Skills Survey
Communication and people skills
The majority of employers value character, interpersonal skills and professional behaviours above formal qualifications when recruiting graduates. Psychology graduates who can demonstrate empathy, professional communication and teamwork are consistently competitive.
CBI/Pearson Education and Skills Survey
Work experience provides a major advantage
Research suggests that internships, prior work experience and professional networks can substantially improve graduate employment outcomes — in some studies rated as more decisive than the degree itself. With only a small proportion of psychology undergraduates gaining relevant work experience during their degree, those who do gain a significant competitive advantage.
Cengage 2025 Graduate Employability Report
Psychology skills in the AI era
The UK Government's AI Labour Market Survey (2025) found that AI roles are increasingly incorporating social sciences including psychology, reflecting demand for human intelligence and ethical expertise. PwC's 2026 AI Jobs Barometer found junior AI-assisted roles are seven times more likely to require human-intensive skills like judgement and leadership.
DSIT AI Labour Market Survey 2025; PwC UK AI Jobs Barometer 2026
