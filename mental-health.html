Mental Health

Clinical, counselling and therapeutic careers — routes into mental health support, psychological therapies and wellbeing services.

Mental health careers are one of the most direct pathways from a psychology degree, spanning therapy, counselling, community support, early intervention, and occupational therapy. Most practitioner roles require further postgraduate training, but there are graduate-entry positions — such as Assistant Psychologist and Mental Health Support Worker — that allow you to build experience and work towards qualified status. Understanding which route suits you early in your degree is an important first step.

Note on training routes: Most clinical and therapeutic roles in this area require postgraduate training and professional registration. Graduate-entry roles such as Assistant Psychologist or Mental Health Support Worker are valuable stepping stones. Each sub-theme below explains what further training is typically needed and when.
What you could do now
  • Volunteer with mental health charities such as Mind, Samaritans, or Rethink to build relevant experience
  • Look into Assistant Psychologist roles and what NHS Trusts expect at application — most require a 2:1 and relevant voluntary experience
  • Explore the NHS Health Careers website for detailed information on each training pathway
  • Research the differences between clinical psychology, counselling psychology, and CBT training routes — they lead to different careers
  • Consider joining the BPS Student Committee or engaging with the Division of Clinical Psychology's student resources

Psychological Therapy — High Intensity

High-intensity psychological therapy roles deliver evidence-based interventions for moderate to severe mental health conditions. These are specialist practitioner roles that require postgraduate training and professional registration, but a psychology undergraduate degree is the typical entry point and provides strong preparation.

Training note: All roles in this area require postgraduate qualifications. A psychology degree provides the academic foundation, but you will need to gain experience in graduate-entry roles (such as Assistant Psychologist or support worker) before applying to training programmes.
Why Psychology?
  • Understanding of psychological models of mental health — including cognitive, behavioural, and psychodynamic frameworks — is foundational to therapeutic practice
  • Research methods training develops the evidence-based thinking that underpins clinical work
  • Communication, empathy, and professional boundary skills developed throughout a psychology degree are directly transferable
  • Academic grounding in psychopathology and assessment prepares graduates for postgraduate clinical training
Graduate entry — where most people start
Assistant Psychologist
Supports qualified clinical or counselling psychologists in assessment, intervention delivery, and research within NHS or charity settings. The most common graduate-entry route into clinical psychology training.
Graduate entry
Qualified practitioner roles — require further training
Clinical Psychologist
Assesses and treats mental health conditions across the lifespan using multiple therapeutic models. Requires a BPS-accredited Doctorate in Clinical Psychology (DClinPsy), which is highly competitive, and registration with the HCPC.
Doctorate required
High-Intensity CBT Therapist
Delivers one-to-one cognitive behavioural therapy for moderate to severe depression, anxiety, and trauma. Requires a postgraduate diploma in high-intensity CBT, usually completed via an IAPT training programme.
Postgrad diploma required
Systemic / Family Therapist
Works with couples and families to address patterns of interaction that affect mental health and wellbeing. Requires MSc-level training in systemic practice, accredited by the Association for Family Therapy.
MSc required
Skills that may be useful
Empathy and active listening Professional boundaries Knowledge of psychopathology Evidence-based practice Written communication Resilience
Example employers
  • NHS Mental Health Trusts — the main employer of clinical psychologists and CBT therapists
  • Improving Access to Psychological Therapies (IAPT) services within NHS primary care
  • Charitable organisations delivering NHS-commissioned mental health services (e.g. Mind, Turning Point)
  • Private mental health clinics and independent therapy practices
What you could do now
  • Research the DClinPsy application process early — competition is high and relevant experience is essential
  • Pursue voluntary work with mental health charities to build a strong experience profile
  • Look at Assistant Psychologist job adverts to understand what experience NHS Trusts expect
  • Explore the Leeds Clearing House website — the central application system for clinical psychology training

Early Intervention — Low Intensity

Low-intensity roles provide brief, structured support for people with mild to moderate mental health difficulties, acting as the first line of response in community and primary care settings. These are among the most accessible graduate-entry pathways into mental health work, with training delivered on the job alongside a postgraduate qualification.

Why Psychology?
  • Knowledge of CBT principles and psychological models of anxiety and depression is directly applied in low-intensity work
  • Communication and interpersonal skills from a psychology degree are central to effective guided self-help delivery
  • Understanding of child development and educational psychology is particularly relevant for school-based roles
  • Research training supports evidence-based approaches to early intervention
Possible roles in this area
Psychological Wellbeing Practitioner (PWP)
Delivers low-intensity, guided self-help interventions for anxiety and depression in NHS primary care settings. Training is completed on the job alongside a postgraduate qualification, typically over one year.
Graduate entry with on-job training
Education Mental Health Practitioner (EMHP)
Works within schools to deliver early mental health interventions to children and young people with emerging needs. A relatively new role, trained via the Children and Young People's IAPT programme.
Graduate entry with on-job training
Children's Wellbeing Practitioner (CWP)
Delivers evidence-based low-intensity interventions for children and young people experiencing mild to moderate mental health difficulties, working within NHS and school-based mental health services.
Graduate entry with on-job training
Skills that may be useful
Active listening Knowledge of CBT principles Empathy Written communication Organisation Resilience
Example employers
  • NHS IAPT (Improving Access to Psychological Therapies) services in primary care
  • NHS Mental Health Trusts running Children and Young People's mental health services
  • Schools and Multi-Academy Trusts with embedded mental health teams
  • Local authority children's services and early help teams
What you could do now
  • Explore NHS Health Careers for detailed information on the PWP, EMHP, and CWP training routes
  • Volunteer with mental health or young people's charities to build relevant experience
  • Research NHS Trust vacancies for trainee PWP roles — these are typically advertised annually
  • Look into the BABCP website for information on low-intensity CBT training and accreditation

Counselling and Psychotherapy

Counsellors and psychotherapists support clients through emotional and psychological difficulties by providing a structured space to explore thoughts, feelings, and behaviour. These roles draw on a range of therapeutic approaches — including CBT, psychodynamic, and humanistic models — and require professional training and accreditation before independent practice.

Why Psychology?
  • Psychology training provides the theoretical grounding in therapeutic models — cognitive, behavioural, psychodynamic, humanistic — that underpins counselling and psychotherapy practice
  • Understanding of human development, attachment, and mental health informs therapeutic approaches
  • Strong communication, empathy, and reflective skills developed through a psychology degree are central to effective therapeutic work
  • Research training supports evidence-based practice and critical evaluation of therapeutic approaches
Protected titles: The titles Clinical Psychologist and Counselling Psychologist are protected by law and can only be used by practitioners registered with the HCPC. By contrast, Counsellor and Psychotherapist are not legally protected titles in the UK — anyone can use them. This means the quality of training and practice can vary considerably. When exploring training routes, look for programmes accredited by BACP or UKCP, and be cautious of unaccredited courses.
Possible roles in this area
Counsellor
Works with clients to explore personal difficulties and support them in finding their own solutions. Found in GP surgeries, schools, universities, charities, and private practice. Requires a recognised counselling qualification and accreditation.
Professional training required
Psychotherapist
Provides longer-term therapeutic support, exploring deeper patterns of thought, feeling, and behaviour. Works from a specific theoretical model — most commonly CBT, psychodynamic, or humanistic. Requires postgraduate training and professional registration.
Postgrad training required
Counselling Psychologist
Integrates psychological research with therapeutic practice, working with clients experiencing a range of psychological difficulties. Requires a BPS-accredited Doctorate in Counselling Psychology and HCPC registration.
Doctorate required
Skills that may be useful
Active listening Empathy Self-reflection Therapeutic knowledge Professional boundaries Ethical practice
Example employers
  • NHS Mental Health Trusts and IAPT services
  • GP surgeries and primary care networks
  • Universities and student counselling services
  • Mental health charities (e.g. Mind, Relate, Cruse Bereavement)
  • Independent and private therapy practice
What you could do now
  • Research the difference between counselling, psychotherapy, and counselling psychology — they involve different training routes and lead to different careers
  • Explore BACP and UKCP accredited training courses for counselling and psychotherapy
  • Consider voluntary listening roles (e.g. Samaritans, university peer support) to develop foundational skills
  • Look into the BPS Qualification in Counselling Psychology if you want to pursue the academic-practitioner route

Community and Social Support

Community and social support roles help people with mental health needs to live independently, access services, and engage with their communities. These roles sit across the NHS, local authorities, and the charity sector, and many are accessible to psychology graduates at entry level — making them an important route into mental health work and further training.

Why Psychology?
  • Understanding of mental health conditions and their social determinants is directly relevant to community support work
  • Knowledge of behaviour change, motivation, and wellbeing informs effective support planning
  • Strong communication and interpersonal skills are central to building trust with clients in community settings
  • Ethical awareness and understanding of safeguarding issues is essential in roles working with vulnerable individuals
Possible roles in this area
Mental Health Support Worker
Provides practical and emotional support to people with mental health needs in community, residential, or clinical settings. A common graduate-entry role and a strong foundation for further training in clinical, social work, or nursing pathways.
Graduate entry
Support Time and Recovery (STR) Worker
An NHS-specific role focused on providing time, support, and practical help to people experiencing mental health difficulties in community settings. A good graduate-entry position for those considering a career in mental health services.
Graduate entry
Peer Support Worker
Uses lived experience of mental health difficulties to support others in their recovery. A growing role across NHS and third sector mental health services, increasingly professionalised with formal training pathways.
Graduate entry
Mental Health Nurse
Assesses, plans, and delivers care for people with mental health conditions across inpatient, community, and crisis settings. Requires a three-year nursing degree (or two-year postgraduate entry for graduates) and NMC registration. A common pathway for psychology graduates entering the NHS.
Nursing qualification required
Mental Health Social Worker
Supports people with mental health needs from a social and environmental perspective, addressing the impact of housing, finances, and relationships on wellbeing. Requires a Social Work degree or postgraduate qualification.
Further qualification required
Recovery Worker
Supports people with mental health or substance misuse difficulties to set goals, build independence, and engage with their community. Many roles are accessible at graduate level with relevant experience.
Graduate entry
Skills that may be useful
Empathy Communication Knowledge of mental health conditions Safeguarding awareness Resilience Person-centred approach
Example employers
  • NHS Mental Health Trusts — community mental health teams, crisis services, and inpatient wards
  • Local authority adult social care and mental health teams
  • Mental health charities including Mind, Rethink Mental Illness, and Turning Point
  • Housing associations and supported living providers
What you could do now
  • Volunteer with local mental health charities such as Mind or Rethink to gain relevant experience
  • Search for Mental Health Support Worker and STR Worker vacancies — many NHS Trusts and charities recruit graduates directly
  • Research Mental Health Nursing entry routes — postgraduate entry programmes allow psychology graduates to qualify in two years
  • Explore routes into Social Work if you want to progress to a qualified practitioner role with a social perspective

Occupational Therapy

Occupational therapists (OTs) working in mental health help people to overcome psychological barriers to everyday living — supporting independence, routine, and meaningful activity as part of recovery. OTs are a core part of NHS mental health teams, and a psychology degree provides strong preparation for the postgraduate or pre-registration training required to qualify.

Why Psychology?
  • Understanding of mental health conditions, motivation, and behaviour change is central to occupational therapy in mental health settings
  • Knowledge of human development and the impact of psychological difficulties on daily functioning informs OT assessment and intervention
  • Person-centred values and communication skills developed through a psychology degree translate directly into OT practice
  • Research training supports evidence-based approaches to occupational therapy intervention
Possible roles in this area
Occupational Therapy Assistant
Supports qualified occupational therapists in delivering activities and interventions with mental health service users. An accessible graduate-entry role that provides experience before or alongside OT training.
Graduate entry
Registered Occupational Therapist (Mental Health)
Works with people experiencing mental health difficulties to support their engagement in meaningful daily activities and independence. Requires an accredited OT degree or postgraduate pre-registration qualification, plus HCPC registration.
OT qualification required
Skills that may be useful
Person-centred practice Understanding of mental health Communication Activity analysis Goal setting Empathy
Example employers
  • NHS Mental Health Trusts — inpatient, community, and crisis teams
  • Secure psychiatric hospitals and forensic mental health services
  • Local authority adult social care teams
  • Mental health charities and supported housing providers
What you could do now
  • Research postgraduate pre-registration OT programmes — these allow psychology graduates to qualify as OTs in two years
  • Look for Occupational Therapy Assistant roles in NHS mental health services to gain direct experience
  • Explore the Royal College of Occupational Therapists (RCOT) website for information on training routes and careers
  • Volunteer in mental health settings to build relevant experience and understanding of OT in practice

Mental Health Research

Mental health research applies scientific methods to better understand mental health conditions, evaluate interventions, and inform clinical practice and policy. Research skills developed through a psychology degree — study design, statistical analysis, literature review, and writing — provide strong preparation for research assistant roles in universities, NHS trusts, and research institutes.

Why Psychology?
  • Research methods training — quantitative, qualitative, and mixed methods — directly transfers to mental health research settings
  • Academic grounding in psychopathology and evidence-based practice is foundational to clinical research
  • Statistical skills and familiarity with software such as SPSS are valued in research roles
  • Dissertation experience provides a concrete example of independent research design and execution
Possible roles in this area
Research Assistant (Mental Health)
Supports academic or clinical researchers in data collection, participant recruitment, literature reviews, and analysis. Roles are available in universities, NHS trusts, and research charities at graduate entry level.
Graduate entry
Clinical Research Practitioner
Supports the delivery of clinical research studies within NHS settings, including participant recruitment, data collection, and regulatory compliance. A growing NHS role that psychology graduates are well placed to apply for.
Graduate entry
Clinical Trials Assistant
Supports the delivery of clinical trials evaluating psychological interventions or mental health treatments, including participant management, data entry, and regulatory compliance.
Graduate entry
Postgraduate Researcher (PhD)
Conducts doctoral research into mental health topics, leading to academic or applied research careers. Funded studentships are available through research councils and NHS research programmes.
Postgrad required
Skills that may be useful
Research methods Statistical analysis Academic writing Attention to detail Literature review Data management
Example employers
  • Universities with psychology and psychiatry departments
  • NHS Mental Health Trusts with research departments (e.g. research-active trusts in the NIHR network)
  • Mental health research charities (e.g. MQ Mental Health Research, Mental Health Foundation)
  • Government research bodies and public health agencies
What you could do now
  • Look for undergraduate research assistant opportunities within your department — these are excellent for building a research CV
  • Explore NIHR (National Institute for Health Research) for information on NHS research careers and funding
  • Use your dissertation as a springboard — strong dissertations can lead to publications and PhD applications
  • Research funded PhD studentships in mental health at universities and through the NIHR doctoral training programme
